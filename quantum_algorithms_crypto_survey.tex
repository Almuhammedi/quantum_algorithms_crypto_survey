\title{\textbf{Quantum Cryptanalytic Algorithms and the Transition to Post-Quantum Cryptography: A Survey of Emerging Threats and NIST PQC Standards.}}
\author{Tariq A, Cybersecurity Department, Sana’a University}
\date{March 2026}

\begin{abstract}
This survey synthesizes the latest scientific literature on quantum algorithms with cryptanalytic impact on contemporary public-key and symmetric cryptography, and consolidates current standardization progress in post-quantum cryptography (PQC). We integrate recent results on resource estimates for Shor-type factoring, Regev's improved quantum factoring framework and its elliptic-curve extension, Grover-based impacts on symmetric primitives, implementation attacks such as KyberSlash, and the status of NIST PQC standards (FIPS~203/204/205 and the developing FIPS~206). We conclude with actionable recommendations for migration, crypto-agility, and risk management against \emph{harvest-now-decrypt-later} adversaries.
\end{abstract}


\section{Introduction}
Modern digital security relies heavily on public-key cryptographic systems whose security is based on the computational difficulty of problems such as integer factorisation and discrete logarithms. However, the development of quantum computing introduces a new class of algorithms capable of solving these problems far more efficiently than classical computers. In particular, algorithms such as Shor's Algorithm and Grover's Algorithm pose significant threats to widely deployed cryptographic systems. In response to these risks, the field of post-quantum cryptography has emerged to design cryptographic schemes that remain secure even in the presence of quantum adversaries. After a multi-year evaluation process, the National Institute of Standards and Technology released the first generation of standardised post-quantum algorithms, including FIPS 203, FIPS 204, FIPS 205, and the forthcoming FIPS 206. This research surveys recent quantum algorithms capable of compromising classical cryptographic systems and examines their implications for the security and deployment of newly standardised post-quantum cryptographic schemes.


\section{Problem Statement}
A cryptanalytically relevant quantum computer (CRQC) would break widely deployed public-key systems based on integer factorization and discrete logarithms. Even before such a device exists, adversaries can mount \emph{harvest-now-decrypt-later} (HNDL) attacks by storing encrypted traffic today for future decryption. Organizations must therefore transition to quantum-resistant algorithms in a timely and risk-informed manner while ensuring secure implementations resistant to classical side channels.

\section{Objectives and Research Questions}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Identify the quantum algorithms that most critically threaten current cryptosystems and summarize state-of-the-art resource estimates.
  \item Review the status and technical properties of NIST-standardized and in-development PQC schemes.
  \item Analyze the practical threat from Grover's algorithm to symmetric encryption and hashing, and discuss recommended parameter choices.
  \item Highlight recent implementation-level attacks (e.g., KyberSlash) and extract lessons for secure PQC deployment.
  \item Provide migration recommendations and a research outlook.
\end{itemize}

\section{Literature Review}
\subsection{Shor-type Factoring and Resource Estimates}
Shor's 1994 algorithm established a polynomial-time quantum method for factoring and discrete logarithms. Modern resource estimates incorporate error correction and layout constraints. A widely cited 2019--2021 estimate by Gidney and Ekerå suggested RSA-2048 could be factored in roughly 8 hours on a device with about 20 million noisy physical qubits under surface-code assumptions. In May 2025, Gidney reported a refined construction using approximate residue arithmetic, yoked surface codes for storage, and magic-state cultivation, reducing the qubit requirement to below one million at the cost of a longer runtime (on the order of a week) under the same physical assumptions. In Feb 2026, a preprint introducing a QLDPC-based \emph{Pinnacle} architecture claimed a further order-of-magnitude reduction (to $\sim$100,000 physical qubits) with significant architectural demands and a longer runtime (month-scale). These advances emphasize that algorithmic and architectural innovation can shift feasibility thresholds even without new physics.

\subsection{Regev's Efficient Quantum Factoring and Elliptic Curves}
Regev (2023/2024) proposed a factoring approach with gate complexity $\tilde{O}(n^{3/2})$ per run and $\tilde{O}(\sqrt{n})$ repetitions, relying on a heuristic number-theoretic assumption, with subsequent analyses and implementations exploring space/noise tradeoffs. In 2025, Barbulescu, Barcau, and Pa\c{s}ol extended Regev's framework to elliptic-curve discrete logarithms (ECDLP) and discussed parameter regimes where it could asymptotically outperform Shor on EC instances, while also delineating instances where Regev may fail for certain RSA moduli and conditions for success under Gaussian heuristics.

\subsection{Grover's Algorithm and Symmetric Primitives}
Grover's algorithm provides a quadratic speed-up for unstructured search, implying that an idealized key search over a $k$-bit key space requires about $2^{k/2}$ evaluations. Practical assessments incorporate circuit depth, parallelization limits, and error-correction overheads; contemporary analyses suggest that while Grover reduces \emph{asymptotic} security, the wall-clock cost remains enormous for well-chosen parameters. Consequently, AES-256 and SHA-384/512 remain robust targets against quantum adversaries, with many policies recommending at least AES-256 for long-lived confidentiality.

\section{Analysis of Quantum Cryptanalytic Algorithms}
\subsection{RSA and Finite-Field DLP}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Shor + surface code baselines.} RSA-2048 factoring feasibility has been linked historically to multi-million-qubit machines; recent work compresses qubit counts by trading space for time and optimizing non-Clifford resources.
  \item \textbf{Under one million qubits.} The 2025 analysis places sub-million-qubit factoring within week-scale runtime under optimistic hardware parameters and aggressive error-correction techniques.
  \item \textbf{QLDPC pathways.} The Pinnacle architecture posits $\sim$100k physical qubits with month-scale runtimes but requires fast QLDPC decoding and non-local connectivity not yet demonstrated at scale.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Elliptic-Curve Cryptography}
Shor's algorithm breaks ECDLP in polynomial time on a CRQC. Regev-style methods may yield asymptotic improvements under stated assumptions, with early indications of feasibility for certain standard curves; however, practical superiority relative to state-of-practice Shor implementations remains unsettled and highly dependent on constants, architectures, and heuristics.

\subsection{Symmetric Ciphers and Hashes}
For symmetric primitives, Grover suggests doubling key length to offset the quadratic speed-up in the idealized oracle model. In practice, the cost model requires considering parallelization inefficiencies and error-correction costs; conservative guidance for long-lived data is to use AES-256 and SHA-384/512, while monitoring evolving physical resource estimates for quantum AES circuits.

\section{Post-Quantum Cryptography and NIST Standards}
\subsection{Finalized PQC Standards (Aug 13, 2024)}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{FIPS 203 (ML-KEM):} Kyber-derived lattice KEM (MLWE), with parameter sets ML-KEM-512/768/1024.
  \item \textbf{FIPS 204 (ML-DSA):} Dilithium-derived lattice digital signatures (Module-SIS/MLWE).
  \item \textbf{FIPS 205 (SLH-DSA):} Stateless hash-based signatures (SPHINCS+), a conservative backup.
\end{itemize}
\subsection{In-Development and Additional Selections}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{FIPS 206 (FN-DSA / Falcon):} NTRU-lattice signatures with compact signatures; draft in preparation with attention to floating-point sampling, validation, and side-channel hardening.
  \item \textbf{Code-based KEMs:} NIST selected HQC (2025) for standardization to diversify KEM families beyond lattices.
\end{itemize}

\section{Results and Discussion}
\subsection{Risk Posture and Timelines}
Recent reductions in qubit estimates for RSA-2048 factoring (from $\sim$20M to $<1$M and potentially $\sim$100k with QLDPC) compress perceived timelines for CRQC feasibility. While these analyses rely on optimistic but not impossible hardware assumptions, their trendline strengthens the HNDL risk model. Long-lived data encrypted today with vulnerable public-key schemes may be at risk if not rekeyed to PQC.

\subsection{Implementation Attacks: KyberSlash}
The KyberSlash timing attacks (KyberSlash1/2) target secret-dependent division timings in some Kyber (ML-KEM) implementations (including reference code prior to late-2023 patches). Practical key recovery was demonstrated on Cortex-A7 and Cortex-M4 under repeated decapsulation, with patches deployed across major libraries. The scheme remains sound; the lesson is that \emph{constant-time, constant-memory-access} implementations and rigorous validation are mandatory for PQC rollouts.

\subsection{Operational Guidance for Symmetric Primitives}
Given Grover's implications and practical cost estimates, organizations should prefer AES-256 for confidentiality with long lifetimes and SHA-384/512 for hashing, while recognizing that Grover does not parallelize linearly and that real-world costs under error correction are far higher than idealized oracle counts.

\section{Conclusions}
Quantum cryptanalysis places asymmetric cryptography on a deprecation path. Rapid progress in algorithmic resource estimates underscores the urgency of migrating to PQC while maintaining secure implementations. The NIST PQC standards (FIPS~203--205) provide stable building blocks; FIPS~206 and HQC will further improve algorithmic diversity.

\section{Recommendations}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Adopt standardized PQC now:} Prioritize ML-KEM (FIPS~203) for key establishment and ML-DSA (FIPS~204) for signatures; use SLH-DSA (FIPS~205) where conservative assurance is needed.
  \item \textbf{Deploy hybrid modes:} Use hybrid (classical+PQC) in protocols (e.g., TLS 1.3) during transition for defense-in-depth.
  \item \textbf{Harden implementations:} Enforce constant-time coding, side-channel testing, and validated libraries; ensure prompt patching (e.g., KyberSlash mitigations) and independent verification.
  \item \textbf{Strengthen symmetric baselines:} Prefer AES-256 and SHA-384/512 for long-lived confidentiality/integrity; reevaluate key lifetimes under HNDL.
  \item \textbf{Build crypto-agility:} Inventory cryptographic dependencies (CBOM), automate certificate lifecycle, and design for algorithm agility.
  \item \textbf{Monitor research:} Track resource-estimate updates (e.g., sub-million-qubit factoring), FIPS~206 progress, and code-based KEM maturation (HQC).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Challenges and Future Research}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Error-correction engineering:} Demonstrating fast, scalable QLDPC decoding and robust fault-tolerant operation.
  \item \textbf{Efficient PQC implementations:} Reducing footprint and energy on constrained devices while maintaining side-channel resistance.
  \item \textbf{Protocol integration:} Optimizing certificate chains, handshake sizes, and fallback strategies with ML-DSA/FN-DSA and ML-KEM/HQC.
  \item \textbf{Cryptanalysis:} Continued scrutiny of lattice assumptions, structured rings, and conservative alternatives (hash-based signatures; code-based KEMs).
\subsection{PQC Immaturity and Lattice Monoculture Risk}
Although the 2024 NIST PQC standards represent a milestone in cryptographic modernization, the ecosystem remains technically immature and disproportionately dependent on lattice-based assumptions. With ML-KEM and ML-DSA forming two of the three finalized NIST algorithms, global deployments risk a lattice monoculture, wherein a single-family vulnerability could trigger systemic collapse across cryptographic infrastructures. This concern is magnified by early evidence of implementation fragility, illustrated by the KyberSlash timing attacks that enabled full secret-key recovery in certain ML-KEM decapsulation implementations. Furthermore, analyses of PQC deployment show lattice-based primitives impose substantial architectural burdens—such as MTU fragmentation and hardware saturation—highlighting the immaturity of infrastructure to support PQC at scale. Strategic surveys emphasize the need for algorithmic diversity and crypto-agility to mitigate correlated-failure risk, leading NIST to introduce HQC and prepare Falcon as alternative standards.

\subsection{Foundational Mathematical Risks}
Cryptography fundamentally relies on assumptions of mathematical hardness rather than proven theorems. Many widely used primitives---including RSA, ECC, and modern hash functions---depend on the belief that certain problems cannot be solved efficiently. However, breakthroughs in the underlying mathematics could invalidate these assumptions entirely. One example is the Riemann Hypothesis (RH), whose resolution could provide deeper insights into the distribution of prime numbers. While a proof alone may not immediately break RSA, associated algorithmic advances could dramatically weaken prime factorization, reducing the security margin of RSA-based systems. Even more catastrophic would be a resolution of the P vs NP question. If it were proven that P = NP, many classes of problems currently thought intractable could be solved in polynomial time, rendering nearly all modern encryption, hashing, and signature schemes insecure, regardless of hardware or quantum capabilities. These foundational risks highlight that cryptographic security is not solely a technological challenge but also a mathematical one, reinforcing the importance of maintaining crypto-agility, algorithmic diversity, and long-term research into hardness assumptions.

\subsection{Lightweight Cryptography and the Absence of FIPS-Approved Stream Ciphers}

A significant challenge in deploying secure cryptography on constrained platforms—such as mobile devices, embedded systems, and industrial control systems (ICS)—is the absence of lightweight, energy-efficient symmetric algorithms within the current NIST FIPS portfolio. Despite the widespread adoption of ChaCha20-Poly1305 in modern Internet protocols, including TLS 1.3, ChaCha20 is not included in any NIST Federal Information Processing Standard (FIPS) and is therefore not permitted in FIPS-validated environments. Independent analyses confirm that ChaCha20 is absent from NIST’s approved list of validated algorithms due to the fact that NIST has not certified any stream cipher for federal use. \cite{nist_chacha20_missing}

Furthermore, multiple expert discussions note that NIST currently approves \emph{no} stream cipher under any FIPS publication. Instead, AES—particularly in authenticated modes such as AES-GCM and AES-CCM—remains the sole FIPS-approved option for symmetric encryption, while legacy algorithms like 3DES are limited to decryption of existing data. \cite{nist_no_streamcipher} This constraint poses difficulties for low-power hardware lacking AES acceleration, where ChaCha20 would normally offer substantial performance and energy efficiency advantages.

Notably, the recently finalized post-quantum cryptography standards (FIPS 203, 204, and 205) do not include any lightweight symmetric primitives. These standards exclusively define post-quantum key establishment and signature schemes (ML-KEM, ML-DSA, and SLH-DSA), with no provision for lightweight encryption suited to constrained environments. \cite{nist_pqc_fips} Similarly, the forthcoming FIPS 206 for FN-DSA (Falcon) focuses exclusively on signatures and does not address lightweight symmetric needs. \cite{nist_fn_dsa}

This gap reveals a strategic limitation in current FIPS-approved cryptography. While ChaCha20-Poly1305 is widely deployed in the commercial cryptographic ecosystem due to its speed and suitability for mobile and embedded systems, regulatory restrictions prevent its adoption in federally regulated systems or any environment requiring validated cryptographic modules. Addressing this discrepancy may require NIST to validate ChaCha20 formally, or to elevate candidates from the NIST Lightweight Cryptography (LWC) project into future FIPS standards, thus enabling safer and more efficient cryptography for constrained environments.\end{itemize}
