\begin{abstract}
The rapid progress of quantum computing presents a potential paradigm shift in the field of cryptography.
Many widely deployed public-key cryptographic systems rely on mathematical problems such as integer
factorisation and discrete logarithms, which are considered computationally infeasible for classical
computers. However, quantum algorithms demonstrate that these assumptions may not hold in the presence
of sufficiently powerful quantum machines.

This research surveys major quantum cryptanalytic algorithms and analyses their potential impact on
classical cryptographic systems. It further reviews the emerging field of post-quantum cryptography (PQC)
and examines the cryptographic standards recently released by the National Institute of Standards and
Technology (NIST), including FIPS 203, FIPS 204, FIPS 205, and the forthcoming FIPS 206.

The study provides an analytical overview of the quantum threat landscape and evaluates the security
assumptions underlying the newly standardised PQC algorithms. In addition, the research discusses
implementation considerations, remaining security challenges, and the broader implications of the
transition toward quantum-resistant cryptographic systems.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Modern digital security relies heavily on public-key cryptographic systems whose security is based on
the computational difficulty of problems such as integer factorisation and discrete logarithms.
However, the development of quantum computing introduces a new class of algorithms capable of solving
these problems far more efficiently than classical computers. In particular, algorithms such as
Shor's Algorithm and Grover's Algorithm pose significant threats to widely deployed cryptographic
systems.

In response to these risks, the field of post-quantum cryptography has emerged to design cryptographic
schemes that remain secure even in the presence of quantum adversaries. After a multi-year evaluation
process, the National Institute of Standards and Technology released the first generation of
standardised post-quantum algorithms, including FIPS 203, FIPS 204, FIPS 205, and the forthcoming
FIPS 206.

This research surveys recent quantum algorithms capable of compromising classical cryptographic
systems and examines their implications for the security and deployment of newly standardised
post-quantum cryptographic schemes.

\section{Problem Statement}

Most currently deployed public-key cryptographic systems rely on computational hardness assumptions
that are believed to be secure against classical computers. However, the emergence of quantum
computing challenges these assumptions. Quantum algorithms have demonstrated the theoretical
ability to efficiently solve certain mathematical problems that underpin classical cryptography.
This raises serious concerns about the long-term security of digital communications, financial
systems, and critical infrastructure that depend on these cryptographic mechanisms.

\section{Literature Review}

Research on quantum cryptanalysis began with the development of algorithms capable of solving
mathematical problems that were previously considered computationally infeasible.
Shor's algorithm demonstrated that integer factorisation and discrete logarithms could be solved
efficiently on a quantum computer. Later, Grover's algorithm showed that quantum computers could
accelerate brute-force search operations.

In response, researchers developed post-quantum cryptographic constructions based on lattice
problems, hash functions, code-based cryptography, and multivariate polynomial systems.

\section{Analysis of Quantum Cryptanalytic Algorithms}

\subsection{Shor's Algorithm}

Shor's algorithm provides a polynomial-time method for factoring integers and computing discrete
logarithms using a quantum computer. As a result, it directly threatens widely deployed
cryptographic systems such as RSA and elliptic curve cryptography.

\subsection{Grover's Algorithm}

Grover's algorithm provides a quadratic speedup for unstructured search problems. Although it
does not completely break symmetric cryptography, it reduces the effective security level of
symmetric keys.
